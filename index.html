The Ultimate Guide to Selling Inherited Property in 2025

Inheriting a home can be a blessing and a burden. One of the biggest questions we get is: "Do I have to pay taxes on a house I inherited?"

The short answer is: Probably not much, thanks to the 'Stepped-Up Basis'.

What is Stepped-Up Basis?

When you inherit a property, the IRS "resets" the value of the home to its fair market value on the date of the previous owner's death. This is called the Stepped-Up Basis.

Example: Mom bought the house in 1990 for $50,000. It was worth $300,000 when she passed away. You sell it for $300,000.

  • Without Step-Up: You'd pay tax on $250,000 gain.
  • With Step-Up: You pay tax on $0 gain.

Should You Renovate or Sell As-Is?

Many heirs calculate the cost of repairs incorrectly. If the house needs a new roof ($15k), HVAC ($8k), and modern kitchen ($25k), you are looking at $48,000 out of pocket before you even list it.

Can One Heir Force a Sale?

Yes. In most states, if heirs generally cannot agree, one heir can file a "Partition Action" lawsuit to force the sale of the property. This is expensive and drains the equity. It is almost always better to agree on a buyout or a sale to a third-party investor.

FAQ

How are proceeds split?
Proceeds are split according to the will or state probate laws (usually equally among children).
Who pays the mortgage while in probate?
The estate is responsible, but if the estate has no cash, the heirs may need to cover it to avoid foreclosure.
